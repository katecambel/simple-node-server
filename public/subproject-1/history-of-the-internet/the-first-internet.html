The First Internet

The TCP assimilated the OSI reference model and gave way to the TCP-IP protocol standard that guaranteed compatibility between networks and merged them, creating the Internet.

In 1969, through the ARPANET, the first message was sent over the internet using the government’s computer. The Advanced Research Projects Agency Network worked with universities to send the first message over the Internet, minimizing the amount of computation by spreading the work on multiple stations. From the University of California, Los Angeles, or simply, UCLA, to Stanford University, they tried to send the message "login" but only the letters "l" and "o" were sent. Nonetheless, that was already a huge deal then. By the end of that year, only 4 computers were connected and by July 1970, there were 9 computers, including Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT) and University of Utah in the mix.

In 1971, the University of Hawaii became a part of the network, and then two years later, it included various networks in London and Norway. On the same year of 1971, Ray Tomlinson was working on sending email between users of ARPANET to send an electronic mail (now more commonly known as e-mail).

In 1973, the first international connection from the US to the University of London, England. Through time, a bunch of networks were developed such as the SATNET, CSNET, MILNET, and NSFNET. The NSFNET or the National Science Foundation Network became the most popular network as it allowed non-university networks to use the network.

On February 28, 1990, ARPANET hardware was removed but Internet was up and running.

Fun fact, the symbol "@" was used to tell user's name and host's name apart.
